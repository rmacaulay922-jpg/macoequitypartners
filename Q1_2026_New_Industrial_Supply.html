New Industrial Developments & Supply Pipeline

Tri-County South Florida: Palm Beach, Broward & Miami-Dade • Q1 2026

Executive Summary

South Florida's industrial construction pipeline is contracting meaningfully heading into mid-2026. After a two-year surge that delivered millions of square feet of new warehouse space, the pipeline has shrunk to approximately 4.2 million SF across the Tri-County area—down from peak levels exceeding 12 million SF in 2023. This matters enormously for existing asset owners: as deliveries decline, absorption will catch up to supply, vacancy will compress, and rent growth will re-accelerate. Critically, virtually none of the remaining pipeline is small-bay product—the new supply is overwhelmingly big-box logistics and distribution (100K+ SF), leaving the sub-15K SF segment structurally undersupplied.

Importantly, this type of product does NOT compete with small-bay—these buildings serve an entirely different tenant base (3PLs, national distributors) at different price points ($12–$15/SF NNN vs. $24–$30/SF NNN for small-bay).

What’s NOT Being Built: Small-Bay

Critical Supply Gap: Virtually no new small-bay industrial (<15,000 SF multi-tenant) is being developed in South Florida. The economics are prohibitive: at current land costs of $40–$80/SF and construction costs of $150–$200/SF, a developer would need to charge $30+/SF NNN just to hit a 6% yield on cost—above current market rents in many submarkets. This means existing small-bay product is effectively irreplaceable, providing structural downside protection for owners.

County-Level Analysis

Miami-Dade County

Miami-Dade still has the largest pipeline at 2.9 million SF under construction, though this is down sharply from peak levels. In Q1 2026, 1.4 million SF was delivered, contributing to a 20 bps increase in vacancy to 7.1%. However, leasing activity remained solid at 3.2 million SF for the quarter, and rental rates continued climbing to $17.04/SF NNN (+1.5% YoY). The Hialeah, Doral, and airport corridors remain the tightest submarkets for small-bay product, with vacancy well below the countywide average.

Broward County

Broward has the smallest remaining pipeline at just 506,434 SF—meaning the supply overhang is nearly resolved. Q1 leasing totaled 1.4 million SF, though absorption remained negative at -303,000 SF as some large tenants downsized. Average asking rents held at $17.21/SF NNN. Pompano Beach and Deerfield Beach continue to attract institutional capital (Blackstone, Kurv, Bain Capital all made acquisitions), signaling investor confidence in the submarket despite near-term absorption softness.

Palm Beach County

Palm Beach has 797,000 SF remaining in the pipeline—a fraction of what was under construction 18 months ago. Q1 leasing was light at 469,000 SF (the lowest in two years), pushing vacancy up 40 bps to 7.2%. However, asking rents leveled off at $15.98/SF NNN rather than declining, suggesting the market is finding a floor. The Lake Worth and Riviera Beach industrial corridors remain particularly tight for small-bay product, with single-digit vacancy and limited turnover.

Implications for Investors

The Supply Story Is Turning: The 65% decline in construction pipeline from 2023 peak levels is the single most important data point for South Florida industrial investors. By year-end 2026, the pipeline will likely drop below 2 million SF—a level not seen since 2021. This sets up a favorable supply-demand dynamic for 2027–2028, particularly for small-bay product where no new supply exists. Owners of well-located, multi-tenant small-bay assets should see accelerating rent growth and compressing cap rates as the market tightens.
Maco Equity Partners Perspective: The declining construction pipeline is the tailwind behind our investment thesis. We target small-bay industrial assets in supply-constrained South Florida submarkets precisely because new development cannot dilute our competitive position. As big-box deliveries wind down and market-wide vacancy stabilizes, the spotlight will shift back to the small-bay segment—where vacancy never rose above 3% and demand never wavered.
