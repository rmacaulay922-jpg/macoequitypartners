What "Patient Capital" Actually Means in 2026

MEP
Maco Equity Partners February 2026 8 min read

Every PE deck has the phrase. Every LP pitch leans on it. "Patient capital" has been worn smooth from overuse, and the firms claiming it the loudest are often the ones with the shortest hold periods and the most aggressive fund-life dynamics. So let's say what we actually mean by it.

Patient capital, in 2026, is not a marketing claim. It is a structural choice that constrains every other decision the firm makes — sourcing, leverage, structure, exit. Here's how we think about each.

1. Hold period is the tell

The single best predictor of whether a firm is actually patient is the distribution of its actual realized hold periods, not the language in its pitchbook. Fund-of-funds PE structures push GPs toward 4–6 year holds, regardless of what the deal economically wants to do. That isn't patience — that's a calendar.

Our average hold period across the assets we still own is approaching nine years. That isn't a feature we marketed up-front; it's the consequence of a structure that doesn't require us to sell. We can sell when the asset has reached its operating peak, when capital markets are accommodating, or when the next-best use of the equity is clearly higher. None of those conditions correlate with a fund-life clock.

2. Leverage is where patience either survives or dies

The cleanest test of whether a sponsor is actually patient is what happens to its portfolio in a refinance window. Aggressive cap structures — bridge loans, short-fuse mezz, leverage above 75% LTV on stabilized assets — turn a patience strategy into a forced-seller strategy the first time the rates curve moves against you.

We size our debt deliberately conservatively for the current rate environment. Going-in LTV is rarely above 60% on real estate, and we underwrite to a debt-service coverage ratio that holds at a stress rate at least 200 basis points above the contract rate. This costs us a few hundred basis points of levered IRR in the base case. It also means a 200 bps move in rates doesn't make us sellers.

What we mean by patient leverage

If the next refinancing event would force a sale at a price below the lower bound of our underwriting band — we're not patient. We're just borrowed-up.

3. Structure has to match the strategy

The fund structure determines whether the principal can be patient even when they want to be. A closed-end fund with a stated 7-year life and three-year extension options is structurally impatient at year five regardless of how the manager talks about hold periods.

We invest through evergreen vehicles and structured separately-managed mandates where the lifecycle of the capital matches the lifecycle of the underlying assets. Our LPs know that "exit" might mean a refinance distribution rather than a sale. That alignment removes the single biggest pressure point that has historically forced GPs into bad outcomes.

4. The deals we walked away from

The best signal of patience is what a firm doesn't buy. Here are three deals we passed on in the last twelve months that another sponsor with our nominal cost of capital would have closed:

  • A 180,000 SF distribution warehouse in Doral, going-in cap of 5.6%. Excellent asset, excellent location. The reason we passed: tenant concentration. A single occupant accounted for 88% of NOI, with three years remaining on the lease. The cap rate didn't compensate for the rollover risk on the single name.
  • A small-bay multi-tenant property in Pompano at a 6.4% cap. The numbers were close to what we underwrite. The reason we passed: 60% of the leases were short-term at materially below market, and the value-add thesis required a year of disruption to mark to market. We could have made the deal pencil with optimism on the lease-up timeline. We chose not to.
  • A growth-equity investment in a regional logistics services business at 6.5x EBITDA. The business was real, the management was strong. The reason we passed: the customer concentration on the top three accounts was 71% and one of those three was already running a competing RFP. A patient capital answer is "not at this price, not at this concentration."

None of these passes felt heroic. They felt like discipline.

5. Patience is not passivity

The most common misuse of the phrase is to use it as cover for inaction. Patient capital is not capital that sits in the bank waiting for the world to come to it. It's capital that has the discipline to wait for the right deal and the operating intensity to compound that deal once it owns it.

"The job isn't to be slower than other capital. The job is to be deliberate about when speed matters and when it doesn't."

The post-close playbook we run is anything but passive. We're in the building, in the books, in the weekly business reviews. The patience is in the hold; the intensity is in the operating.

Why this matters for the next cycle

We expect the next 24–36 months to surface a meaningful gap between firms that have nominal patient-capital marketing and firms whose structural choices actually let them hold through volatility. The refinance wall in commercial real estate alone is going to be a sorting mechanism. So is the LP appetite for shorter-duration vehicles in an environment where shorter duration consistently underperforms.

If you're an LP evaluating sponsors right now, the question to ask isn't "Are you patient?" The honest answer is always yes. The question is: What's your actual realized hold period distribution? Followed by: Show me the deals you didn't do this year.

The answers tell you everything.
